by Claudia Lujan

Posted on April 22, 2014 at 1:08 PM

The Problem of Racial Categories

Recent Census data has placed the Latino population as the largest ethnic majority in the United States. With an astounding 53 million individuals, the group constitutes 17% of the total population and is expected to grow to 29% by 2050. As with other ethnic and racial minorities, Latino populations are afflicted with certain diseases at disproportionate ratesâ€”an indicator of health disparities partly resulting from language/culture barriers and access to health insurance. With cancer topping the list for leading causes of death, efforts directed toward early diagnosis and heath care access are a promising strategy to improving mortality rates. Care must be taken, however, to move away from generalized assumptions when addressing health care disparities.

Deciphering health care disparities of the Latino population, especially in the treatment of diseases such as cancer, is confounded by the racial and ethnic diversity that exists within this umbrella term. Though statistical information is useful in conceptualizing overall trends and patterns, it often fails to provide the specifications needed for successful intervention. If public health efforts are to be effective, interventions must be tailored to address specific health care risks. For example, recent data has shown that the age-adjusted rates of cancer in Florida for Cuban men (327.5 per 100,000) are twice as high as those of Mexican men (163.4 per 100,000). As just one of the many examples that direct our attention toward variation within the Latino population, care must be taken to approach health care disparities as a phenomenon that can exist beyond the confines of our categorizations.
